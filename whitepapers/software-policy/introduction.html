Introduction

The benefits of computerized health records are indisputable, and further benefits are expected from the implementation of computerized decision support. However, current systems are only implementing a small fraction of the potential benefits. The government has been called upon to finance development of software projects aiming at improving health outcome and saving costs to the health system.

However, there are no guidelines as to which projects to support, under what conditions. So far, ?? Australian taxpayers dollars have been spent on ?? projects, arguably without any major tangible outcome. While each project in itself may have its merits, the lack of interoperability and reusability reduced the long term benefits of such software close to zero. Since virtually all projects purchased or commissioned software without getting access to its source code, that software cannot even serve for learning purposes for our upcoming generations of health informaticians. We may conclude that most of the project money spent so far on software has been wasted.

Current common practice is to put a software project to tender, and have a small set of end user specifications implemented by a vendor. Typically, implementation details are not specified and source code remains property of the vendor. Even in the few cases where the public ownership of the source code was specified in the contract, the tender missed to specify that availability of source code needs to extend to 3rd party libraries and all non-replaceable software tools. (The software must be extensible and maintainable without any requirements for proprietary 3rd party libraries or tools).

In the worst case, monopolies are fostered by the choice of vendors which misuse such publicly funded projects in order to lock customer into their proprietary solutions, effectively not only depriving customers of a choice but also choking the competition which might have been able to offer better alternatives in the future. 'Once a customer is "locked into" proprietary software, its makers can demand premium prices, safe in the knowledge that the client would find it even more expensive to change' [1,2]

It is also essential to involve clinicians throughout all phases of planning and decision making regarding health related software:
"Health care IT is led almost exclusively by commercial vendors and health care corporate management information services (MIS) departments. There is a poverty of clinician leadership.
Even medical informaticists, clinicians ideally qualified to provide such leadership, often find themselves poorly accepted in vendor and MIS shops. This may be due to territorial issues, cultural differences, and methodological differences. One such methodological difference is the clinician's favoring of a participatory approach to application development, which represents a significant departure from traditional information system design.This may make those in industry or MIS uncomfortable." [25,26]

In order to maximize benefits from publicly funded software, a minimum policy should be implemented. This document is aiming at providing a rationale for such a policy.
